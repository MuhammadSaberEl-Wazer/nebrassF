The history of Fasting

Ramadan is that the ninth month of the Islamic calendar , and is noted because the month of fasting.
During this month, most Muslims actually fast everyday from Dawn till sunset. This fast includes abstinence from food, drink, smoking, and sexual activities.
Furthermore, foul talk, lustful thoughts, and vain speech is even more discouraged than normal.
Islam is based on five pillars. These pillars include:
Testifying there's no God deserve worship besides Allah, and Muhammad is His messenger.
Prayer five times each day at the appointed times.
Fasting during the month of Ramadan.
Giving a portion of one's wealth in charity to the poor and needy,
because Allah provides us with a lot of things so we have to give the less fortunate some of his blessings.
Making a pilgrimage to the Kaaba in Mecca a minimum of once during a lifetime, if one is able financially, mentally and physically.
Fasting is that the third pillar and features a vital role in Islam. While Muslims may fast throughout the year,
fasting during the month of Ramadan is an obligatory action a bit like praying and giving charity.
However, some people are exempt from this obligation. This is discussed further below.
Muslims are commanded to fast in the Quran. Allah tells us: (You who believe, fasting is prescribed on you as it was prescribed on those before you, so that you may learn self-restraint).

When Allah says 'those who came before you,' he's pertaining to Jews and Christians since they follow books and prophets that came before the Quran and Muhammad.
Therefore, Allah is telling us that Jews and Christians were ordered to fast also .
There is ample evidence of this statement in both the Old Testament and the New Testament. Moses fasted while on the mountain for forty days and forty nights.
In order to avoid punishment from God as prophesied by Jonah, the people of Nineveh fasted.
Even Jesus fasted for forty days and forty nights within the desert.
We can see that fasting was practiced well before the time of Prophet Muhammad and therefore the establishment of the faith of Islam.
Jews still fast on certain days of the year, and a few sects of Christianity, most notably Catholics and Orthodox Christians, also fast on certain days.
However, only a few Protestant denominations mandate fasting, though many do encourage it among their faithful.
The Islamic fast has many benefits.
For example, since most fasting is completed during a particular month, there's a way of community and belonging. It is nothing in need of amazing that over a billion people the planet over are all fasting at an equivalent time.
Furthermore, even though both rich and poor must fast, the wealthy amongst us have the opportunity to feel some of the hardship experienced by those less fortunate.
In addition to the present , since Ramadan is additionally referred to as the month of charity,
the poor even have the prospect to realize from the improved benevolence of the rich during this time.
